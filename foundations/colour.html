          Colour

          This page defines the colour roles used across Gen 2 Media Sites and how those roles are expressed in different contexts.

          Overview

          Colour defines how hierarchy, meaning and emphasis are communicated across Gen 2 Media Sites.

          The colour system prioritises clarity, consistency and accessibility to support content-heavy and media-rich experiences.

          • Contexts – where colour appears and the visual environment it operates within
          • Roles – what a colour is for, independent of palette or brand

          This separation allows colour to adapt to different visual environments without changing intent, hierarchy or meaning.

          Client branding and site-specific adjustments are documented under White labelling.

          Colour contexts

          Contexts describe where colour appears and the visual environment it operates within. Standard is the default reading environment. Emphasis is used where additional prominence is required. In both, role meaning and hierarchy remain unchanged.

          Colour roles

          Base colours

          Purpose

          Base colours provide the structural foundation of the interface. They dominate the UI and establish hierarchy, legibility and stability.

          Used for

          • Page backgrounds
          • Content surfaces (cards, panels)
          • Borders and dividers
          • Primary and secondary text

          Base colours in different contexts

          Standard context

          • Prioritises stability and low visual noise
          • Defines the default reading environment
          • Supports long-form content and dense layouts

          Emphasis context

          • Uses a more prominent surface environment
          • Contrast may increase to preserve readability
          • Hierarchy and meaning remain unchanged

          What can change: surface tone and contrast. What cannot: hierarchy, intent and role meaning.

          Action colours

          Purpose

          Action colours communicate interactivity and affordance. They should clearly signal available actions without overwhelming surrounding content.

          Used for

          • Buttons and button states
          • Text links
          • Interactive controls and focus indicators
          • Hover and active feedback for interactive elements

          Action colours in different contexts

          Standard context

          • Affordance is clear without dominating content
          • Links remain distinct from body text
          • Interactive states remain predictable

          Emphasis context

          • Contrast may increase against prominent surfaces
          • Interactive elements remain identifiable
          • Action meaning remains unchanged

          What can change: contrast and tone to suit the surface. What cannot: affordance and hierarchy.

          Semantic colours

          Purpose

          Semantic colours communicate system meaning and status, such as success, warning, error and informational states. They convey outcomes and feedback rather than interaction or structure.

          Used for

          • Success, warning, error and information messages
          • Status indicators and alerts
          • Validation feedback and system responses

          Semantic meaning must remain consistent across the system and should never rely on colour alone. Text, icons and structure must reinforce meaning.

          Rules

          Colour meaning must remain consistent across contexts, and themes may adapt values without changing role intent.

          • Roles define what colour means. Contexts define where it appears.
          • Roles do not change between standard and emphasis contexts. Only contrast may adjust.
          • Do not rely on colour alone to communicate meaning. Use text, icons or structure as reinforcement.

          Role reference

          Colour roles describe purpose across the interface. Values and palette mappings are theme-specific.

          Role What it communicates
          Base Structure, hierarchy and stable surfaces for reading and layout
          Action Interactivity and affordance for controls, links and focus states
          Semantic Status and meaning such as success, warning, error and information

          Themes may adjust values and brand expression, but role meaning and hierarchy must remain consistent.

          Accessibility and usage

          Colour must support inclusive use across all contexts.

          • Maintain sufficient contrast for text and interactive elements.
          • Reinforce semantic meaning with text, icons or structure rather than colour alone.
          • Keep colour usage predictable to support recognition and comprehension.

          Summary

          • Gen 2 colour separates context from role to maintain consistent meaning.
          • Roles define what colour means. Contexts define where it appears.
          • Themes may adapt values, but not semantic meaning or hierarchy.

          Gen 2 colour uses defined roles and contexts to communicate hierarchy, interaction and semantic meaning consistently across the interface.
